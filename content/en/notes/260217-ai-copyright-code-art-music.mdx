The perceived difference in how AI "stealing" plays out across creative domains comes down to power dynamics, not rules.

**Code** has licenses ([MIT](https://opensource.org/licenses/MIT), [GPL](https://www.gnu.org/licenses/gpl-3.0.en.html)), but AI companies largely ignore them under "Fair Use." [GitHub Copilot](https://github.com/features/copilot) is a closed-source product trained on copyleft code. [*Doe v. GitHub/Microsoft*](https://githubcopilotlitigation.com/) is still being fought. Developers tolerate it because we're pragmatic and used to remixing ([StackOverflow](https://stackoverflow.com/) culture).

**Visual art** is the most contentious because illustrators work solo. AI directly replaces the commission economy. You can't copyright a style, only specific works. Images on the web rarely carry "do not train" metadata. Individual artists have no leverage.

**Music** looks quiet but isn't. The [RIAA](https://www.riaa.com/) sued [Suno](https://suno.com/) and [Udio](https://www.udio.com/) in June 2024. The industry has the most powerful lawyers in the world. [UMG](https://www.universalmusic.com/) and [WMG](https://www.wmg.com/) are already signing licensing deals with AI companies. The future is "licensed generation" where artists get pennies ([Spotify](https://www.spotify.com/) model).

The difference: coders share by culture and AI exploits that. Artists are atomized individuals losing to automation. Musicians belong to massive conglomerates that force AI companies to pay. The rules are being written in courtrooms, not in code.
